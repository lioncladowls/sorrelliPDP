Every decision in this cart redesign started with a question: does this element help the customer commit, or does it give them a reason to pause? The cart is the last place a shopper deliberates before checkout. We designed both surfaces — the slide-out drawer and the dedicated /cart page — to meet customers exactly where they are in that decision.

1. The Hybrid Approach

1

Why Both a Drawer and a /cart Page

The Problem With a Single Surface

Most ecommerce stores pick one: a slide-out drawer cart, or a dedicated cart page. Both have tradeoffs. The drawer keeps shoppers on the product page — great for momentum, bad for complex decisions. The /cart page gives room to deliberate — great for high-AOV purchases, bad for impulse buys.

Sorrelli customers often buy with intent. They've browsed, considered, and chosen. But they also respond to discovery — adding a second piece after seeing the first in context. We needed both surfaces optimized for their specific role.

Each Surface Has a Job

The drawer is a momentum-preserving confirmation. Its job is to acknowledge the add and route the customer efficiently toward checkout without breaking their browsing flow. The /cart page is a deliberation surface — where a customer reviews their order, confirms totals, applies promotions, and makes the final commitment.

The Data

Internal A/B data from comparable jewelry brands shows drawer-first carts produce +17% add-to-cart engagement (customers continue browsing and adding), while dedicated cart pages show +13% checkout completion (customers who arrive are more committed). Running both surfaces — with "View Full Cart" in the drawer — captures both effects.

When Each Surface Appears

The drawer and cart page serve different intents, so they're triggered differently:

  • "Add to Cart" button → Opens the drawer. The customer just made a decision. The drawer confirms it, offers a path to checkout, and lets them continue browsing. This is a momentum moment — don't break it with a full page redirect.
  • Cart icon click → Navigates to the /cart page. Clicking the cart icon is a deliberate signal: "I want to review my order." That intent deserves the full deliberation surface — totals, express checkout, promo codes, gift options, cross-sell.

This pattern is validated by CRO testing across comparable DTC brands: drawers hurt mobile conversion by -8.4% when used as the primary cart surface, but excel as transient add-to-cart confirmation. The /cart page is where considered-purchase shoppers — Sorrelli's core audience — make their final commitment.

2. Drawer Philosophy: Ruthless Restraint

2

The Drawer's Job Is to Get Out of the Way

The Core Principle

The drawer opens only when a customer adds an item to cart — never from the cart icon. Its job is to confirm the action, show the path to checkout, and get out of the way. We stripped it to exactly what's necessary: a free shipping progress bar, line items with quantity controls, subtotal, shipping cost, BNPL line, the primary CTA, a compact trust line, a gift text link, and a "View Full Cart" link.

What We Removed From the Drawer

  • Cross-sell recommendations. Baymard research shows even one irrelevant recommendation destroys trust in all recommendations. Without AI-curated matching (e.g., Rebuy), generic cross-sell in the drawer violates the restraint principle. Competitive brands closest to Sorrelli (Gorjana, Mejuri, Ana Luisa) keep drawers lean with no cross-sell. Cross-sell lives exclusively on the /cart page where customers are in a deliberation mindset. Exception: if Sorrelli adds Rebuy AI, 1-2 collection-matched items could return to the drawer.
  • Gift checkbox + modal. NNGroup progressive disclosure research: in speed surfaces, options that don't apply to the majority create cognitive load even when the user decides "no." A checkbox is 3 UI elements (box + label + info icon) that 80% of self-purchasers must process. Replaced with a single tertiary text link — "Sending as a gift? See options →" — routing to the /cart page where the full gift option card lives. This serves the 20% who need it without burdening the 80% who don't.
  • Promo code field. On mobile, expanding a promo input opens the keyboard, disrupts scroll position, and sends 8% of users off-site hunting for a code they may never find. The resulting abandonment is measurable. Promo codes live on the /cart page — a deliberation surface where customers expect to spend time.
  • Delivery estimate. Pre-checkout delivery timing is addressable on the /cart page and at checkout. In the drawer, it's a distraction — and a potential hesitation point if timelines are uncertain.
  • Full trust strip. A row of badges mid-drawer competes with the CTA for visual attention and adds scroll depth. Condensed to a single trust line below the CTA.

Industry Models

The most conversion-efficient drawers in retail — Glossier, Gorjana, Allbirds — share a common trait: they are nearly empty. Item, total, button. Anything else is optional decoration.

Mobile Behavior

On mobile, the drawer CTA becomes a sticky footer pinned to the bottom viewport edge, no taller than 160px. The cart list scrolls freely above it. This keeps the primary action permanently accessible regardless of cart depth.

The drawer is not a mini cart page. It's a confirmation and routing surface. The more it tries to do, the less it converts.

3. Cart Page Sidebar: Conversion Flow

3

The Sidebar Order Is Intentional

The Sequence

The /cart page sidebar flows from top to bottom in a deliberate conversion sequence: Express checkout → Order Summary → Estimated Total → BNPL → Primary CTA → Post-decision reassurance (delivery estimate, trust strip, promo code).

This order reflects the customer's mental state at each point. They arrive wanting to know their options (express checkout). They scan the summary to confirm accuracy. They see the total, then immediately see a way to make it smaller (BNPL), then see the button to act.

Total → BNPL → CTA: An Unbroken Flow

These three elements are treated as a single unit. The total anchors the price. The BNPL line immediately reframes it into a smaller number. The CTA captures the decision before hesitation sets in. Placing anything between these three — a promo field, a badge row, a policy note — breaks the momentum we've built.

Post-Decision Reassurance

The delivery estimate, trust strip, and promo code all appear below the CTA. This is intentional. For the decisive customer, they never need to see them — the CTA is already clicked. For the hesitant customer who scrolls past the CTA, these elements provide the reassurance needed to scroll back up and commit. Placing them above the CTA would make them pre-decision hesitation triggers instead.

Promo Code: Accessible but Not Prominent

Promo codes are collapsed behind a subtle "Have a promo code?" text link, not a prominent card or expanded input. Prominent promo fields cause 8-12% of customers to leave in search of a code. Collapsed fields serve customers who have one without advertising the gap to those who don't.

4. Free Shipping Progress Bar

4

Addressing the #1 Abandonment Reason

Why It Leads Both Surfaces

Unexpected shipping costs are the single most cited reason for cart abandonment — cited by 48% of abandoning shoppers in Baymard's checkout usability research. Showing shipping status at the top of both the drawer and the /cart page eliminates the most common surprise and reframes "I'm spending money" as "I'm working toward a reward."

The $150 Threshold

The free shipping threshold is set at $150, approximately 20-30% above Sorrelli's average order value. This is the range recommended by conversion research: close enough to be achievable, high enough to meaningfully lift AOV. Thresholds set too close to AOV are left on the table; thresholds set too far are ignored as aspirational.

Dynamic State Design

The bar is never static. In-progress: truck icon with copper accent color, "{$X} away from free shipping" copy. Qualified: checkmark icon with muted blue accent, "You've unlocked free shipping!" copy. The state change uses smooth CSS transitions — the visual reward of the bar filling is itself a micro-motivation to add one more item.

The Lift

Progress-bar-based free shipping nudges increase threshold conversion by 15-25% in controlled tests. For a jewelry brand with well-defined product categories, this is one of the highest-ROI cart interventions available.

Shipping & Tax in the Order Summary

Beyond the progress bar, both surfaces show the actual shipping cost in the order summary: FREE when the threshold is met, $9.95 when it isn't. Tax shows as "Calculated at checkout" — the industry-standard line used by Amazon, Mejuri, Gorjana, and every major retailer. This is honest: sales tax requires a shipping address to calculate, and shoppers understand this. The critical thing is setting the expectation that tax will be added, not surprising them at checkout. Between the progress bar and the explicit shipping line, both of the top two abandonment triggers — "extra costs too high" (48%) and "couldn't see total cost up-front" (21%) — are addressed before the customer ever reaches checkout.

5. Product Status Transparency

5

Every Item Shows Its Status Inline

The Four States

Each cart line item displays its fulfillment and policy status at a glance:

  • In Stock — Green dot indicator. Confirms availability without noise.
  • Clearance / Final Sale — Rose-gold pill badge + "Final Sale — not eligible for returns" note directly below the item. No surprises at checkout or after delivery.
  • Excluded from Discount — Small info icon with a neutral, plain-language explanation ("This item is excluded from promotional discounts"). Removes checkout confusion without apology.
  • Low Stock — Amber "Only 2 left" label. Honest urgency, not manufactured scarcity.

Why the Cart Is the Right Place

Per Baymard Institute and Nielsen Norman Group research, omitting item-level restrictions from the cart — and surfacing them only at checkout or post-purchase — is one of the most trust-destroying experiences in ecommerce. Customers feel deceived. We show restrictions early, in the cart, in plain language, so there are no checkout surprises and no return-policy complaints after delivery.

Tone Matters

Restriction notices use neutral, factual language — not apologetic ("Sorry, this item can't be returned") or legalistic ("Terms apply"). Plain-language honesty builds more trust than over-caveated or softened copy.

Trust is built by showing what the customer needs to know before they reach checkout — not by hiding it and hoping they won't notice.

6. BNPL Strategy

6

Afterpay Only, Inline, Simple

Why Afterpay Only

Afterpay accounts for approximately 96% of Sorrelli's BNPL transactions. Shop Pay Installments and other providers are used by fewer than 4% of customers — and their visual presence creates noise that dilutes the Afterpay message. We show Afterpay, we show the installment amount, and we stop there.

The Format

A single inline line: "or 4 payments of $X with Afterpay." No modal, no widget, no badge grid. The installment amount is calculated dynamically from the cart total. This format removes price anxiety for jewelry AOV — a $160 necklace at $40/installment reads as substantially more accessible.

The Impact

BNPL availability has been shown to increase AOV by 30-50% and overall conversion by 30-35% in Stripe and Afterpay's published research across fashion and accessories. For a brand with AOV in the $75-200+ range, this is not a nice-to-have — it is a primary conversion lever.

What We Removed

We removed Shop Pay Installments (redundant given transaction data), a secondary BNPL badge row, and the "OR" divider that previously appeared between express checkout and the order summary. The divider had no functional purpose and added visual clutter to an area that needs to breathe.

7. Trust Signals: Less Is More

7

Max Three Badges, Below the CTA

The Three We Kept

We kept exactly three trust signals, placed below the primary CTA: 30-Day Returns, Secure Checkout, and 40+ Years. Each addresses a specific hesitation a jewelry buyer might have at the point of committing: "Can I return it?", "Is my payment safe?", and "Is this brand legitimate?"

Position: Below, Not Above

Trust signals appear below the CTA, not above it. For customers who are already ready to buy, they scroll past without adding friction. For customers who hesitate on the CTA and scroll down, the badges provide the final nudge to scroll back up and commit. Placing them above the CTA delays decisive action; placing them below serves hesitant action.

We Removed the Policy Info Strip

The previous design included a horizontal policy strip above the CTA: "Free shipping on orders over $150 · 30-day returns on most items* · Secure checkout." We removed it entirely for two reasons:

  • Redundancy. "Free shipping" was addressed by the progress bar at the top of the page. "Secure checkout" and "30-day returns" were covered by the trust badges below the CTA. The strip added no new information.
  • The asterisk. "30-day returns on most items*" introduces ambiguity at precisely the wrong moment. The asterisk tells a customer "your item might not qualify" without telling them why. This is a purchase anxiety trigger. The strip was doing net harm.

When a trust signal also introduces doubt — like an asterisk on a return policy — it's doing more harm than good. Remove it.

8. What We Deliberately Left Out

8

Every Removal Is Research-Backed

Removed: Savings Callout Box

A highlighted "You're saving $X" block competed with the CTA for visual attention and introduced a second focal point in the conversion flow. Customers who were comparing savings instead of clicking checkout converted at lower rates. Savings are visible in line-item strikethrough pricing — they don't need a dedicated block.

Removed: Dual BNPL Providers

Showing both Afterpay and Shop Pay Installments created a provider-comparison micro-decision at the worst possible moment — right before checkout. With 96% of BNPL transactions on Afterpay, the second provider was visual noise. One provider, one message.

Removed: "OR" Divider

The horizontal "OR" divider between express checkout and the order summary was a skeuomorphic holdover from checkout page design. In the sidebar context, it served no functional purpose and added visual weight that fragmented the conversion flow.

Removed: Policy Info Strip

Detailed above in section 7. Redundant with other elements on the page, and the asterisk on return policy was a net negative for conversion.

Removed From Drawer: Cross-Sell

Generic cross-sell recommendations were removed from the drawer entirely. Baymard research demonstrates that even one irrelevant recommendation destroys trust in all recommendations. Without AI-curated matching (e.g., Rebuy), static product suggestions violate the drawer's core principle as a confirmation surface. Competitive brands closest to Sorrelli — Gorjana, Mejuri, Ana Luisa — keep drawers lean with no cross-sell. Cross-sell lives exclusively on the /cart page. If Sorrelli adds AI-powered recommendations in the future, 1-2 collection-matched items could return to the drawer.

Removed From Drawer: Gift Checkbox

The gift checkbox (checkbox + label + info icon) was replaced with a single tertiary text link: "Sending as a gift? See options →" that routes to the /cart page. NNGroup progressive disclosure research shows that in speed surfaces, options that don't apply to the majority create cognitive load even when the user decides "no." 80%+ of Sorrelli customers are self-purchasers — the checkbox was 3 UI elements that the vast majority had to process and dismiss. The full gift experience lives on the /cart page sidebar, where deliberation is expected.

Removed From Drawer: Promo Code

On mobile, the promo input opens the keyboard and disrupts the scroll state. More importantly, displaying a promo field to customers who don't have a code signals that codes exist — sending approximately 8% of users off-site to find one. Most never return. Promo codes are available on the /cart page, where the customer is already in a deliberation mindset.

These decisions are grounded in Baymard Institute checkout research, Stripe's BNPL study, and analysis of top-performing jewelry brands (Mejuri, Gorjana). Full research is available in the Cart Best Practices Research document.
